## SECTION 1: DEPARTMENT OVERVIEW

The Neurology & Neurosurgery Department provides comprehensive care for disorders of the brain, spinal cord, nerves, and muscles, with a specialized Neuro-Oncology unit for brain and spinal tumors. The department includes a 10-bed Neuro ICU (NICU), a dedicated Tumor Board (multidisciplinary team), and access to Gamma Knife radiosurgery via partnership with the Oncology department.

Q: What services does the department offer?
A: Services include general neurology consultations, EEG (Electroencephalogram), EMG/NCS (Electromyography/Nerve Conduction Studies), MRI/CT-guided diagnostics, neurosurgery (craniotomy, tumor resection, spine surgery), stereotactic biopsy, Gamma Knife radiosurgery coordination, stroke management (thrombolysis), epilepsy management, and a dedicated Brain Tumor Clinic.

Q: What are the OPD hours and location?
A: Neurology/Neurosurgery OPD operates Monday-Saturday, 8:00 AM - 6:00 PM, located on the 2nd Floor, B Wing (Rooms N-301 to N-305). The Brain Tumor Clinic runs Tuesdays and Fridays, 10:00 AM - 1:00 PM (Room N-305), with the multidisciplinary Tumor Board meeting every Friday afternoon.

## SECTION 3: BRAIN TUMOR - PATIENT EDUCATION FAQs

Q: What are the common types of brain tumors?
A: Brain tumors are broadly classified as:
- **Primary tumors** (originate in the brain): Gliomas (including Glioblastoma, Astrocytoma, Oligodendroglioma), Meningiomas, Pituitary Adenomas, Schwannomas (e.g., Acoustic Neuroma), Medulloblastomas (more common in children).
- **Secondary/Metastatic tumors**: Cancer that has spread to the brain from another organ (lung, breast, kidney being common primary sites).
Tumors are also classified as benign (non-cancerous, slow-growing, e.g., most meningiomas) or malignant (cancerous, aggressive, e.g., glioblastoma).

Q: What are common symptoms that may indicate a brain tumor?
A: Symptoms depend on tumor location and size but commonly include: persistent headaches (often worse in the morning), new-onset seizures, progressive weakness or numbness on one side of the body, vision or speech difficulties, balance/coordination problems, personality or cognitive changes, and nausea/vomiting unrelated to other causes. These symptoms warrant prompt neurological evaluation, not necessarily indicating a tumor, but require investigation.

Q: How is a brain tumor diagnosed?
A: Diagnosis typically involves:
1. Clinical neurological examination
2. MRI Brain with contrast (gold standard imaging) - shows tumor location, size, and characteristics
3. CT Brain (often used in emergency settings or if MRI is contraindicated)
4. Biopsy (stereotactic or during surgery) - provides definitive histopathological diagnosis and grading
5. Additional tests: MR Spectroscopy, Perfusion MRI, or PET scan for further characterization

Q: What is "tumor grading" and why does it matter?
A: The WHO grading system classifies brain tumors from Grade 1 (slow-growing, less aggressive, e.g., pilocytic astrocytoma) to Grade 4 (fast-growing, most aggressive, e.g., glioblastoma). Grading guides treatment planning (extent of surgery, need for radiation/chemotherapy) and helps estimate prognosis.

Q: What are the treatment options for brain tumors?
A: Treatment depends on tumor type, location, grade, and patient factors, and may include:
- **Surgery (Craniotomy)**: Maximal safe resection is often the first step for accessible tumors
- **Radiation Therapy**: Including conventional fractionated radiotherapy or Gamma Knife/stereotactic radiosurgery for smaller, well-defined tumors
- **Chemotherapy**: Particularly for high-grade gliomas (e.g., Temozolomide for glioblastoma)
- **Targeted Therapy/Immunotherapy**: For specific molecular subtypes (based on genetic testing of tumor tissue)
- **Observation ("Watchful Waiting")**: For small, asymptomatic, slow-growing tumors (e.g., small meningiomas in elderly patients)
A multidisciplinary Tumor Board (neurosurgeon, oncologist, radiation oncologist, pathologist, radiologist) reviews each case to recommend the optimal treatment plan.

Q: What is a craniotomy and what does recovery involve?
A: A craniotomy is a surgical procedure where a portion of the skull is temporarily removed to access the brain for tumor removal or other treatment. Recovery typically involves 1-2 days in the Neuro ICU, followed by 5-7 days in the ward, with physiotherapy and rehabilitation as needed. Full recovery can take 6-12 weeks, with follow-up MRI scans to monitor for residual tumor or recurrence.

Q: What is Gamma Knife radiosurgery?
A: Despite its name, Gamma Knife involves no actual cutting. It delivers precisely focused beams of radiation to a small, well-defined target (such as a small brain tumor, metastasis, or arteriovenous malformation) in a single session or a few sessions, minimizing damage to surrounding healthy brain tissue. It is often used for tumors that are small, deep-seated, or in surgically risky locations.

UHID: VIH-2026-004523 | Name: Arjun Verma | Age/Sex: 45/M
Chief Complaint: Progressive headache for 6 weeks, worse in mornings, with one episode of seizure (generalized tonic-clonic) 3 days ago.

Diagnosis: Right frontal lobe mass, likely high-grade glioma (provisional, pending histopathology)
Plan: Started on Dexamethasone (to reduce cerebral edema) and Levetiracetam (anti-seizure medication). Case presented at Tumor Board. Plan for awake craniotomy with maximal safe resection, intraoperative neuro-monitoring, followed by histopathology and molecular profiling (IDH mutation status, MGMT methylation) to guide adjuvant therapy.
Outcome: Surgery performed; gross total resection achieved. Histopathology confirmed Glioblastoma (WHO Grade 4), MGMT methylated (favorable for Temozolomide response). Patient referred to Oncology for adjuvant radiotherapy + Temozolomide chemotherapy (Stupp protocol). NICU stay: 2 days; total hospital stay: 9 days.

## SECTION 6: NICU AND TUMOR BOARD INFRASTRUCTURE

Q: What is the bed capacity and equipment in the Neuro ICU (NICU)?
A: The NICU has 10 beds equipped with ICP (Intracranial Pressure) monitoring systems, ventilators, continuous EEG monitoring capability, and dedicated neuro-critical care trained nursing staff (nurse-to-patient ratio 1:1 for ventilated patients, 1:2 otherwise).

Q: What is the Tumor Board and how often does it meet?
A: The Tumor Board is a multidisciplinary team comprising neurosurgeons, medical/radiation oncologists, neuroradiologists, and pathologists that meets every Friday afternoon to review complex neuro-oncology cases and formulate consensus treatment plans. Patients/families are informed of the Board's recommendation typically within 24-48 hours of the meeting.

## SECTION 7: NEUROLOGY/NEUROSURGERY DEPARTMENT FAQs

Q: How urgently should a patient with new-onset seizures be evaluated?
A: New-onset seizures, especially in adults, warrant prompt evaluation (within 24-48 hours) including MRI brain and EEG, as they can be the first sign of structural brain abnormalities including tumors, requiring timely diagnosis and management.

Q: Is genetic/molecular testing done for brain tumors at this hospital?
A: Yes, for gliomas, molecular markers including IDH mutation status, MGMT promoter methylation, and 1p/19q co-deletion are tested on resected tumor tissue (processed in-house or sent to partner reference labs), as these significantly influence prognosis and treatment choice (e.g., chemotherapy responsiveness).

Q: What support services are available for brain tumor patients and families?
A: The hospital offers a Neuro-Oncology Counselor for psychological support, a Patient Navigator to help coordinate appointments across Neurosurgery/Oncology/Radiology, and connects families with patient support groups (e.g., Brain Tumor Foundation of India) upon request.

Q: How soon after surgery can a patient expect to receive the final histopathology report?
A: Standard histopathology takes 5-7 working days. If molecular/genetic profiling (IDH, MGMT, etc.) is included, the complete report may take 10-14 working days, as some molecular tests are sent to external reference laboratories.

## SECTION 9: EPILEPSY SURGERY PROGRAM

Q: Does the hospital offer surgical treatment for drug-resistant epilepsy?
A: Yes, patients with epilepsy uncontrolled by at least two appropriate anti-epileptic drugs undergo a comprehensive pre-surgical evaluation including video-EEG telemetry, MRI epilepsy protocol, and neuropsychological assessment, reviewed by the Epilepsy Board before considering resective surgery or vagal nerve stimulation.

Q: What is video-EEG telemetry and how long does it take?
A: Video-EEG telemetry involves continuous simultaneous video and EEG recording, typically over 3-5 days of hospital admission, to capture and correlate seizure events with electrical brain activity, helping localize the seizure focus for potential surgical planning.

## SECTION 10: STROKE UNIT DETAILS

Q: Does the hospital have a dedicated Stroke Unit?
A: Yes, an 8-bed dedicated Stroke Unit is co-located with the Neuro ICU, offering continuous cardiac/neuro monitoring, rapid-access CT/MRI, and a specialized stroke nursing team trained in thrombolysis protocols and early rehabilitation.

Q: What is the door-to-needle time target for thrombolysis (tPA) at this hospital?
A: The hospital targets a door-to-needle time of under 45 minutes from ER arrival for eligible acute ischemic stroke patients, supported by the "Code Stroke" fast-track protocol.

Q: Is mechanical thrombectomy available for large vessel occlusion strokes?
A: Yes, in coordination with the interventional neuroradiology team, mechanical thrombectomy is offered for eligible large-vessel-occlusion stroke patients presenting within the extended treatment window (up to 24 hours in select cases based on imaging criteria).

## SECTION 11: NEUROLOGY/NEUROSURGERY FAQs (CONTINUED)

Q: How does the hospital manage low-grade, asymptomatic meningiomas found incidentally?
A: Small, asymptomatic meningiomas are typically managed with "watchful waiting" — serial MRI monitoring at 6 months and then annually — with intervention (surgery or radiosurgery) considered only if growth or new symptoms occur.

Q: Is awake craniotomy available for tumors near eloquent brain areas (speech/motor)?
A: Yes, awake craniotomy with intraoperative brain mapping is performed for tumors located near critical speech or motor areas, allowing real-time functional testing during resection to maximize tumor removal while preserving neurological function.

## SECTION 12: NEUROSURGERY OPERATING ROOM AND EQUIPMENT

Q: What specialized equipment supports neurosurgical procedures at the hospital?
A: The dedicated Neuro-OT (Operating Theatre) is equipped with an intraoperative MRI-compatible suite (for select cases), neuronavigation systems (frameless stereotaxy) for precise tumor localization, intraoperative neuromonitoring (motor/sensory evoked potentials), an operating microscope with fluorescence-guided resection capability (5-ALA for glioma margins), and a CUSA (Cavitron Ultrasonic Surgical Aspirator) for tumor debulking.

Q: What is fluorescence-guided surgery and how does it help in glioma resection?
A: Patients are given an oral agent (5-aminolevulinic acid) preoperatively that causes malignant glioma tissue to fluoresce pink/red under specialized microscope light, helping the surgeon distinguish tumor from normal brain tissue in real time and improve the extent of safe resection.

## SECTION 14: NEURO-REHABILITATION SERVICES

Q: What rehabilitation support is available after stroke or brain/spine surgery?
A: The hospital's Neuro-Rehabilitation unit offers physiotherapy (motor recovery, gait training), occupational therapy (activities of daily living), speech and swallowing therapy, and cognitive rehabilitation, with an individualized plan developed by a multidisciplinary rehab team within 48 hours of neurological stabilization.

Q: Is outpatient rehab available after discharge?
A: Yes, patients can continue outpatient rehab sessions (typically 3-5 sessions/week initially, tapering based on progress) at the hospital's Rehab Center (Ground Floor, C Wing), with home-based exercise programs provided for patients unable to travel frequently.

## SECTION 17: NEUROLOGY/NEUROSURGERY FAQs — FAMILY AND CAREGIVER SUPPORT

Q: How does the hospital support families making decisions about high-risk brain surgery?
A: The neurosurgeon and Neuro-Oncology Counselor hold a dedicated pre-operative counseling session explaining risks, expected benefits, and alternatives in plain language, with written consent obtained only after all questions are addressed; families may request additional time or a second opinion before proceeding.

Q: Are support groups available for long-term epilepsy or brain tumor patients?
A: Yes, the hospital facilitates monthly support group meetings (in partnership with local patient advocacy organizations) for epilepsy and brain tumor patients/families, plus an online community forum moderated by the Neuro-Oncology Counselor for ongoing peer support between visits.

Q: What is the hospital's policy on providing copies of imaging (MRI/CT discs) for second opinions elsewhere?
A: Patients can request a copy of their imaging studies (on CD/DVD or via secure digital transfer) from the Radiology department at any time, typically issued within 30 minutes to 2 hours, at a nominal fee of Rs. 100-200 per study, to support second-opinion consultations at other institutions.
