## SECTION 1: DEPARTMENT OVERVIEW

The Cardiology Department at Vihara Multispeciality Hospital provides comprehensive cardiac care including preventive cardiology, diagnostics, interventional cardiology, electrophysiology, and cardiac rehabilitation. The department operates a dedicated Cardiac Catheterization Lab (Cath Lab) and a 12-bed Cardiac ICU (CICU).

Q: What services does the Cardiology Department offer?
A: Services include ECG, 2D Echocardiography, Treadmill Test (TMT), Holter Monitoring, Coronary Angiography, Angioplasty (PTCA with stenting), Pacemaker implantation, Electrophysiology Studies (EPS), Cardiac CT/MRI coordination, and Cardiac Rehabilitation programs.

Q: What are the operating hours of the Cardiology OPD?
A: Cardiology OPD operates Monday-Saturday, 8:00 AM - 6:00 PM, located on the 1st Floor, A Wing (Rooms C-201 to C-204). Emergency cardiac cases are handled 24/7 via the ER and Cath Lab on-call team.

## SECTION 3: COMMON CARDIAC CONDITIONS - PATIENT EDUCATION FAQs

Q: What is Coronary Artery Disease (CAD)?
A: CAD occurs when the coronary arteries (blood vessels supplying the heart muscle) become narrowed or blocked due to plaque buildup (atherosclerosis), reducing blood flow to the heart and potentially causing chest pain (angina) or heart attack (myocardial infarction).

Q: What are common symptoms of a heart attack that should prompt immediate ER visit?
A: Chest pain or pressure (often radiating to the left arm, jaw, or back), shortness of breath, cold sweats, nausea, lightheadedness, and unexplained fatigue. Any of these symptoms warrant immediate emergency care — patients should call the hospital's 24/7 emergency line or go directly to the ER.

Q: What is an ECG (Electrocardiogram) and why is it done?
A: An ECG records the electrical activity of the heart and helps detect arrhythmias, evidence of heart attack (current or past), and other heart abnormalities. It is a quick, painless test taking about 5-10 minutes.

Q: What is the difference between an Angiography and an Angioplasty?
A: Angiography is a diagnostic procedure using contrast dye and X-ray imaging to visualize blockages in coronary arteries. Angioplasty (PTCA) is a therapeutic procedure where a balloon catheter (often with a stent) is used to open the blocked artery. Angiography may lead directly to angioplasty if a significant blockage is found ("diagnostic-to-therapeutic" same-sitting procedure).

Q: What is a Treadmill Test (TMT) / Stress Test?
A: TMT evaluates how the heart performs under physical exertion by monitoring ECG changes, blood pressure, and symptoms while the patient walks on a treadmill at increasing speed/incline. It helps diagnose CAD, assess exercise tolerance, and evaluate treatment effectiveness.

Q: What pre-procedure preparation is required for an Angiography?
A: Patient should fast for 6 hours prior, inform the doctor of any kidney issues (due to contrast dye risk) or allergies, continue prescribed medications unless instructed otherwise (some blood thinners may need adjustment), and arrange for a 1-day hospital stay for observation.

Q: What is cardiac rehabilitation and who needs it?
A: Cardiac rehabilitation is a supervised program of exercise training, education, and counseling for patients recovering from heart attack, angioplasty, bypass surgery, or heart failure. It typically spans 8-12 weeks and aims to improve cardiovascular fitness and reduce recurrence risk.

## SECTION 6: CATH LAB AND CICU INFRASTRUCTURE

Q: How many Cath Labs does the hospital have and what equipment is available?
A: The hospital has 2 Cath Labs equipped with biplane fluoroscopy systems, IVUS (Intravascular Ultrasound), and IABP (Intra-Aortic Balloon Pump) support, capable of performing both diagnostic and interventional procedures including complex PCI and electrophysiology studies.

Q: What is the bed capacity of the Cardiac ICU (CICU)?
A: The CICU has 12 beds, each equipped with continuous cardiac monitoring, ventilator support, and defibrillators. Nurse-to-patient ratio in CICU is maintained at 1:2.

## SECTION 7: CARDIOLOGY DEPARTMENT FAQs

Q: How soon can a patient get an appointment for a routine cardiology consultation?
A: Routine consultations are typically available within 1-3 days. Urgent referrals from other departments (e.g., positive stress test, abnormal ECG) are prioritized for same-day or next-day slots.

Q: Does the hospital provide second-opinion consultations for cardiac surgery?
A: Yes, patients with surgical recommendations from elsewhere can request a second opinion. Reports (angiography films, echo reports) should be brought for review; a separate consultation fee applies.

Q: What is the typical hospital stay duration after angioplasty?
A: Uncomplicated angioplasty typically requires 1-2 days of hospital stay (1 day CICU observation + 1 day ward, or same-day discharge in select low-risk cases performed via radial access).

Q: Is fasting required before all cardiac tests?
A: Fasting (6-8 hours) is required for Angiography, Lipid Profile blood tests, and some Stress Echo protocols. ECG, 2D Echo (routine), and Holter monitoring do not require fasting.

## SECTION 10: PEDIATRIC AND STRUCTURAL HEART FAQs

Q: Does the Cardiology department manage congenital heart disease?
A: Yes, the Pediatric Cardiology clinic (Dr. Sameera Fatima, Wed/Sat) manages congenital conditions such as Atrial Septal Defect (ASD), Ventricular Septal Defect (VSD), and Patent Ductus Arteriosus (PDA), with device closure procedures performed in the Cath Lab for suitable cases, and surgical referral for complex defects.

Q: What follow-up is required after CRT-D or pacemaker implantation?
A: Device checks are scheduled at 1 month, 3 months, and then every 6 months, involving device interrogation to assess battery life, lead function, and therapy delivery, along with wound site review at the first follow-up.

## SECTION 11: CARDIOLOGY DEPARTMENT FAQs (CONTINUED)

Q: Is a dietitian consultation included as part of cardiac rehabilitation?
A: Yes, cardiac rehab includes mandatory dietitian sessions covering sodium restriction, heart-healthy diet planning, and weight management, integrated with the supervised exercise component.

Q: How does the hospital manage patients on blood thinners who need urgent non-cardiac surgery?
A: The Cardiology team provides a "bridging protocol" in coordination with the surgical team, temporarily adjusting or substituting anticoagulants (e.g., switching Warfarin to heparin bridging) to balance bleeding risk against clot risk around the surgical window.

## SECTION 13: PACEMAKER AND DEVICE IMPLANTATION PROGRAM

Q: What types of cardiac devices does the hospital implant?
A: The department implants single and dual-chamber pacemakers, Implantable Cardioverter Defibrillators (ICDs), and Cardiac Resynchronization Therapy devices (CRT-P/CRT-D) for heart failure patients with conduction abnormalities, all performed in the Cath Lab under local anesthesia with sedation.

Q: How long is the hospital stay for a standard pacemaker implantation?
A: Typically 1-2 days: the procedure day plus one day of monitoring, with a wound check and device interrogation before discharge, and a follow-up device check scheduled at 1 month.

## SECTION 14: CARDIAC CT AND ADVANCED IMAGING

Q: Does the hospital offer CT Coronary Angiography (CTCA) as a non-invasive alternative?
A: Yes, CT Coronary Angiography is available via the Radiology department for patients with low-to-intermediate CAD risk, offering a non-invasive assessment of coronary artery calcification and blockages without the need for arterial catheter access, typically used to rule out significant CAD before considering invasive angiography.

## SECTION 16: CARDIOLOGY FAQs — LIFESTYLE AND PREVENTION

Q: What dietary advice is commonly given to cardiac patients?
A: General guidance includes reducing sodium intake (under 2,300 mg/day, or under 1,500 mg/day for heart failure patients), limiting saturated and trans fats, increasing fiber and omega-3 intake, and moderating alcohol consumption, individualized by the cardiac dietitian based on the patient's specific condition.

Q: What exercise recommendations are typically given after a cardiac event?
A: Following medical clearance and completion of the initial cardiac rehab phase, patients are generally advised 150 minutes/week of moderate-intensity aerobic activity (e.g., brisk walking), with specific intensity and progression guided by the cardiac rehab team based on stress test results.

Q: Does the hospital run community cardiac screening camps?
A: Yes, the department conducts free/subsidized cardiac screening camps quarterly at partner corporate offices and community centers, offering basic ECG, BP checks, and risk assessment, with referral pathways to the OPD for abnormal findings.
